Photo Archive 1899-1927




These are a selection of photos/postcards showing village life through the ages.

C 1899: Is this the oldest one"?

Just when you think you've found the oldest village picture (below) this comes along...

This must be the earliest post card of Caerwent village. The house on the left is Box Tree Cottage as it was before it became Frank Drower's bakery, and the occupant, a Mr Lewis, is digging the garden that was to become the forecourt of Caerwent Post Office. On the right, the Old Ship Inn is open for business and climbing up the hill is what appears to be a young girl in a voluminous Victorian dress. The date? Certainly late nineteenth century - lets say 1899.

The same view taken in February 2001 is on the enlargement page for comparison purposes.


C 1900: Earliest days - ' The Caerwent Cross'.

These stones were excavated in the late nineteenth century and raised to the surface where they were rebuilt. This would date this card from the earliest years of the twentieth century, if not before. The overall colouring and lettering bear a distinct similarity to those cards commissioned by Williams & Marshall of Sudbrook in the 1890’s.

C 1913: Another early series of post cards - The Caerwent Village Hill'.

In what must one of the earliest post cards of the village a barrel of water is being pulled up the hill from the pump at the bottom of the village by bakers Ernie Morgan and Frank Drower, who was to open a bakery of his own on his return from the First World War.

The picture was printed from the original 1913 glass negative, which somehow survived among Frank Drower's effects. Clicking to view the enlargement illustrates the advantage of being able to work from such a source - a further enlargement can be made of a small section of the negative, and provides a picture from within the picture.

Unusually, we can be fairly sure of the date of these cards - see below!

C 1913: Another early series of Caerwent post cards - The newly built Burton Homes'.

From the way the title is written we can deduce that this another of the 1913 post cards.

The dedication stone of the Burton Homes is still visible over the door of the central hall and is also dated '1913'.

The card was sent by a youthful Frank Drower to "Miss Lizzie Wood", and the cancellation date over the stamp is January 23rd  1913. Frank was to go to war the following year, returning briefly in 1917 when he married Lizzie. On his discharge from the Royal Garrison Artillery in 1918 he founded his own bakery in the village.

C 1913: The Caerwent Football Team, 1913-1914 Season.

Information is a bit sketchy so far... All that has been discovered is that the picture includes Charlie Hillier, village blacksmith Crady Gorton and the team captain is village baker Frank Drower. Investigations are in progress!

C 1920: W. Adam's Bakery at Caerwent.

This view actually hasn't changed much over the years - the big difference being the construction of the Post Office at the top of the hill in 1932.

The three bakers looking out of the door are thought to be Frank Drower, Ernie Morgan and Cyril Rowe.

1925: Runston Farm, Crick.

Not a photograph this time - an oil painting. Sam Wood, farmer and horse breeder, lived at Runston farm up until his death in 1930. In 1925 he commissioned a local artist (unfortunately unknown) to paint a picture of his beloved Runston. Measuring an impressive three feet by two feet, upon his death in 1930 it passed to his niece Grace Foxley, a resident of the Burton Homes. Grace died in 1999, and the painting is now owned by Sam Wood's grand-daughter, Marjorie Morgan.


C 1925: The Cross, Caerwent.

These stones were discovered at a depth of about three feet, and were raised to the surface and reassembled at the modern ground level. Today they form the supporting plinth for the village War Memorial. Note the long disappeared patch of grass!


1927: The Burton Homes,Caerwent - Founder's Day.

Written on the back of the print is "Tea at the Burton Homes, June 1927." The homes were founded in 1917 by local benefactor, Colonel C.H. Burton, who created the Burton Trust to provide charitable accommodation for elderly ladies. Today the residents may be male or female, and occupation is on a more commercial basis.

C 1930: "Caerwent - the house at the corner"

Amazingly, this rather battered postcard of the village in the snow is the only photographic proof we could find that a house ever existed on the corner! The house, at the extreme right of the picture, was presumably demolished to make way for the eventual excavations of the east Roman wall. No trace remains of the house today. The notice board in front of the wooden hut said "F. Hicks, Builder". The "today" picture shows some interesting variations.
